General Relativity

Gravity Is Not a Force. It's the Shape of Spacetime.

In 1915, Einstein finished a decade-long climb toward a single, radical idea: that gravity isn't a pull between objects, but the curvature of space and time itself.

1915 · Berlin Field equations Confirmed 1919

Imagine a bowling ball dropped onto a stretched rubber sheet. It sinks, and the sheet curves around it. Roll a marble nearby and it doesn't travel in a straight line anymore — it curves toward the ball, not because the ball is "pulling" it, but because the sheet itself is no longer flat.

That, in essence, is what Einstein proposed for the universe. Space and time are not a fixed, rigid backdrop that things happen inside of. They are a single, flexible fabric — spacetime — and mass and energy stretch and dent that fabric. Planets don't orbit the sun because the sun reaches out and grabs them. They orbit because the sun curves the spacetime around it, and the planets are simply following the straightest possible path through a bent landscape.

The decade it took to get here

Einstein's special theory of relativity, finished in 1905, had already shown that space and time were relative to the observer. But it left out gravity entirely. For ten years afterward, Einstein wrestled with a deceptively simple question: how does gravity fit into a universe where space and time can stretch?

The breakthrough came from what he later called his "happiest thought" — imagining a person in free fall. That person feels weightless, as if gravity had simply switched off. Einstein realized that gravity and acceleration might be two faces of the same coin — a principle he called the equivalence principle, and the seed of everything that followed.

"The happiest thought of my life."
— Einstein, describing the moment he first connected gravity to free fall

The field equations

By November 1915, Einstein had the finished mathematics: a set of ten interlocking equations describing exactly how mass and energy curve spacetime, and how that curve in turn dictates how mass and energy move.

Gμν + Λgμν = (8πG / c⁴) Tμν Spacetime curvature (left) equals the matter and energy filling it (right)

It's often summarized as: matter tells spacetime how to curve, and curved spacetime tells matter how to move. The equations are notoriously difficult to solve, but their consequences are enormous — they predict black holes, the expansion of the universe, gravitational waves, and the bending of starlight around massive objects.

Proof, four years later

Einstein's theory made a testable prediction: light passing near the sun should bend by a precise, calculable amount — twice what older Newtonian physics would predict. In 1919, astronomer Arthur Eddington travelled to the island of Príncipe to photograph a solar eclipse, the only moment starlight near the sun's edge could be measured.

The starlight had bent exactly as Einstein predicted. Newspapers around the world ran the story, and overnight, Einstein became the most famous scientist on Earth.

Why it still matters

General relativity isn't just abstract math. Every GPS satellite orbiting Earth experiences time slightly differently than clocks on the ground, because it sits in a weaker gravitational field — exactly as Einstein's equations describe. Left uncorrected, that tiny mismatch would make GPS positions drift by miles within a single day.

A century later, the same equations predicted gravitational waves — ripples in spacetime caused by colliding black holes — which were finally detected directly in 2015, a hundred years almost to the month after Einstein first wrote them down.

Special Relativity

The Speed Limit That Bends Space and Time

In 1905, a 26-year-old patent clerk asked what would happen if you chased a beam of light. The answer rewrote the rules of space, time, and mass — and gave the world its most famous equation.

1905 · Bern The "miracle year" E = mc²

Einstein started with a single, unbreakable rule: the speed of light in a vacuum is the same for every observer, no matter how fast they're moving. It sounds modest. It is not. That one rule forces space and time to stop being fixed and absolute — and to start bending around the speed of light instead.

Picture two friends: one standing on a train platform, one riding past on a train moving near the speed of light. If a flash of light goes off in the middle of the train car, the rider sees it reach both ends at the same instant. But the person on the platform sees the train moving into the light's path on one side and away from it on the other — so to them, the light reaches the back of the car first. Both observers are correct. Simultaneity itself depends on how you're moving.

Time dilation and length contraction

Once simultaneity breaks, time and distance follow. A clock moving relative to you ticks slower, from your point of view — an effect called time dilation. An object moving relative to you appears shorter along its direction of motion — length contraction. Neither is an illusion or a trick of light; both are genuine features of how space and time behave at high speed.

These aren't measurable in everyday life because we move far too slowly compared to light for the effects to show up. But they are very real, and very measurable, for particles in accelerators, astronauts on the space station, and the GPS satellites orbiting overhead right now.

"Put your hand on a hot stove for a minute, and it seems like an hour. Sit with a pretty girl for an hour, and it seems like a minute. That's relativity."
— Einstein, explaining relativity in plain terms

Mass and energy are the same thing

The theory's most famous consequence followed almost as an afterthought, in a short paper Einstein published just months later. If energy and momentum had to transform the way space and time did, then mass itself had to be a form of energy.

E = mc² Energy equals mass times the speed of light, squared

The constant c² is enormous — roughly 90,000,000,000,000,000 in SI units — which is why even a small amount of mass corresponds to a staggering amount of energy. It's the principle behind nuclear power and, more darkly, nuclear weapons; it's also why the sun can shine for billions of years by converting just a sliver of its mass into light every second.

Why "special"?

Einstein called it "special" because it only applied to a special case: observers moving at constant speed, with no acceleration or gravity involved. It would take Einstein another decade of work to extend these ideas to accelerating frames and gravity itself — the leap that became general relativity.

A patent clerk's miracle year

Einstein wrote the special relativity paper, along with three other landmark papers — on the photoelectric effect, Brownian motion, and mass-energy equivalence — all within a single year, 1905, while working full-time as a patent examiner in Bern, Switzerland. Historians now call it his "miracle year." He didn't yet have an academic position; the physics world had barely heard of him. Within a few years, that would change completely.
